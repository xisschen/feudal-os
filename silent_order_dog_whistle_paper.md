# The Silent Order: Digital Dog Whistles and the Media Isomorphism of Feudal Power Structures

## Abstract

The digital "dog whistle" — a single utterance carrying two sets of information, decoded differently by different audiences — is typically treated as a modern communication phenomenon. This paper proposes a transhistorical structural mapping: the logic of dog whistles is not a new invention, but the media migration of feudal power semiotics. Through a systematic one-to-one alignment of four structural variants of digital dog whistles (authorless, hollow, negative-space, recursive) with four core symbolic mechanisms of Zhou Dynasty feudalism (*li*/ritual, *mingfen*/positional naming, *bihui*/taboo naming, *daotong*/orthodoxy), this paper demonstrates that the "immune evasion" capability of digital dog whistles derives from a power-semiotic architecture completed three millennia ago. Three supplementary analytical dimensions — power direction (top-down vs. bottom-up), media materiality (bamboo slips vs. algorithmic recommendation), and the explicit/implicit distinction (public code vs. hidden signal) — further refine the framework. The paper also introduces the *Chunqiu* brushstroke technique, *weiyan dayi* (profound meaning in sparse words), *chenwei* (prognostication), *tuogu gaizhi* (reform through antiquity), and Chan koans as historical evidence of a fully developed premodern Chinese dog-whistle operating system. The conclusion: the ceiling of dog-whistle evolution is not technological — it is the power semiotics finalized three thousand years ago.

**Keywords:** dog whistle; feudalism; power semiotics; media isomorphism; Chunqiu brushstroke; ritual order

## 1. Introduction

Since 2017, major social media platforms have progressively tightened content moderation systems targeting hate speech and disinformation. The result: violative content has not disappeared — it has become quieter. Political YouTubers use cleaner language than a decade ago, yet cause ten times more damage. Scholars refer to this phenomenon as the "dog whistle": a creator emits a seemingly innocuous message, and a specific audience decodes an entirely different meaning.

Existing dog-whistle research remains largely confined to the communication studies paradigm: treating it as a message-encoding strategy and investigating how it survives and spreads under social media algorithms. But this analysis overlooks a deeper question: **the dog whistle works not because it "hides something," but because it rests on an older architecture of power semiotics — one completed three thousand years ago, for which the digital age merely swapped out the transmission medium.**

This paper's core argument is that four structural variants of digital dog whistles — authorless, hollow, negative-space, recursive — bear a systematic structural isomorphism with four core symbolic mechanisms of Zhou Dynasty feudalism — *li* (ritual), *mingfen* (positional naming), *bihui* (taboo naming), *daotong* (orthodoxy). This is not coincidence. It is not metaphor. The two systems implement the same power-semiotic logic using different media materials. Understanding this isomorphism is the key to understanding why dog whistles evolve faster than any content moderation system: the moderation system is racing against an opponent that completed its evolution three millennia ago.

## 2. Literature Review: The Blind Spot in Dog-Whistle Research

### 2.1 Existing Approaches

Dog-whistle research falls into three broad orientations:

**The communication studies approach** treats the dog whistle as a rhetorical strategy. Safire (1988) first introduced "dog whistle" into political communication discourse, describing politicians using seemingly neutral words in public to convey exclusionary information to specific groups. López (2014) analyzed the linguistic features of racialized dog whistles in contemporary American political discourse — ambiguous word choice, multi-interpretable referents, and plausible deniability for the sender.

**The sociological approach** focuses on the group function of dog whistles. Henderson & McCready (2018) argue that the dog whistle is essentially an "in-group signaling" mechanism: its purpose is to identify allies rather than transmit information. This explains why dog whistles often carry no substantive content — the signal is the content.

**The computer science approach** attempts to automatically detect dog whistles using NLP methods. This line of research faces a fundamental difficulty: the semantic analysis of dog whistles depends on context and cultural background, both of which are invisible to machines. Multimodal detection (combining visual, vocal, and textual signals) has made some progress (Sharma et al., 2022) but remains unable to capture "the meaning of absence" — precisely the most critical type of dog whistle.

### 2.2 The Gap

All three approaches share a common blind spot: **they treat the dog whistle as a "new" phenomenon — a product of the digital age or a byproduct of political polarization.** Yet the core structure of the dog whistle has existed in human societies for millennia. Without tracing it back to the deep power semiotics on which it rests, our understanding remains at the level of symptoms.

This paper attempts to fill that gap.

## 3. Core Framework: The Four-Morphism Mapping

This section presents the paper's core theoretical framework: a one-to-one structural mapping between four structural variants of digital dog whistles and four symbolic mechanisms of Zhou Dynasty feudalism.

| Dog Whistle Form | Feudal Mechanism | Structural Isomorphism | Contemporary Case |
|-----------------|------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Authorless | *Li* (Ritual) | Signal origin anonymized; accountability loop severed | TikTok hashtag self-organized substitution |
| Hollow | *Mingfen* (Positional Naming) | Transmits no command, only positional marker | Twitter no-comment retweet |
| Negative-Space | *Bihui* (Taboo Naming) | Silence encoded as signal carrier | Deliberate country omission in commentary list |
| Recursive | *Daotong* (Orthodoxy) | System critique recycled as system fuel | YouTube "I'm just asking questions" |

### 3.1 Authorless Dog Whistle → *Li* (Ritual)

The **authorless dog whistle** refers to a signal structure in which the double-layered meaning of a message is not designed by any specific sender but emerges spontaneously through repeated decoding and re-encoding across different audience groups. There is no "original author" to trace, because the signal emerges from group interaction itself.

*Li* (ritual) in Zhou Dynasty feudalism possesses precisely the same formal characteristics. *Li* is not a decree issued by any monarch — it has no author, no signature, no promulgation date. *Li* is a set of implicit behavioral norms, maintained through intergenerational imitation and daily practice. "This is how everyone does it" — the phrase itself is the operational mode of *li*'s power. No one can answer "who stipulated this?" — because that person cannot be found. The author of *li* has been deliberately erased, or never existed from the start.

The structural isomorphism lies here: **the anonymization of signal origin severs the accountability loop.** For both the authorless dog whistle and *li*, you cannot ask "who said this" — because the answer to that question does not exist. What you can see is only the signal itself and its constraining effect.

### 3.2 Hollow Dog Whistle → *Mingfen* (Positional Naming)

The **hollow dog whistle** transmits no substantive information whatsoever. Its entire meaning is this: the sender and receiver simultaneously confirm each other as "one of us." The signal content is zero — "hello," "nice weather," "we are all patriots" — but the act of sending the signal itself completes the in-group verification.

*Mingfen* (positional naming) in Zhou ritual performs the identical function. "Let the ruler be ruler, the minister be minister, the father be father, the son be son" is not a command — it demands no specific action. *Mingfen* does exactly one thing: **it establishes positional relations within a hierarchical structure.** Once the names and positions of ruler-minister-father-son are fixed, commands become redundant — because you already know where you stand, who he is, and how you should treat him.

Neither the hollow dog whistle nor *mingfen* transmits orders. They transmit **positional markers**. Once position is confirmed, behavior is automatically constrained — there is no need to say "this is what you must do."

### 3.3 Negative-Space Dog Whistle → *Bihui* (Taboo Naming)

The **negative-space dog whistle** is defined not by what is said, but by what is **deliberately left unsaid**. A political commentator recounts global conflicts, listing ten nations but conspicuously skipping the eleventh. The average viewer does not notice the omission; the target audience instantly decodes the silence. "I never mentioned that name" — but the absence is louder than any presence.

*Bihui* (taboo naming) in post-Zhou Chinese society is the formal institutionalization of the same logic. The emperor's personal name could not be written; the ancestors' names could not be spoken. Every literate person in the empire performed daily acts of avoidance in their writing — no one said "I fear the emperor," yet every taboo substitution precisely completed this signal. Silence was institutionalized. *Bihui* transmits no information — *bihui* transmits the **existence of order**.

The structural isomorphism lies here: **silence is encoded as a signal carrier.** Omission is not negligence — omission is intentional, directional, and precisely decodable by the target audience. What must be spoken aloud is susceptible to censorship; what is pointedly left unspoken — the censorship system cannot know is missing, because censorship systems read content, not absence.

### 3.4 Recursive Dog Whistle → *Daotong* (Orthodoxy)

The **recursive dog whistle** is the terminal form of the structure: the content of the dog whistle is the very fact that "someone is using dog whistles." The signal no longer points to an external object — it points to the signaling system itself. "My enemies use dog whistles against me" — this declaration is itself a dog whistle. You do not need any specific political position, because the position of "we" has already been constructed by the narrative of "we are fighting against dog whistles."

*Daotong* (orthodoxy) in Confucian political philosophy is the paradigmatic example of this same recursive structure. The narrative of *li beng yue huai* — "ritual has collapsed, music is corrupted, the world is in decline, we are losing orthodoxy" — itself becomes the strongest fuel maintaining the system. *Daotong* does not need to describe a specific enemy — the enemy is "whoever might be destroying orthodoxy." Whoever declares "orthodoxy is collapsing," whether they realize it or not, is performing the reproduction of orthodoxy. Just as a YouTuber who declares "I've been targeted by dog whistles" — whether they realize it or not — is manufacturing a new dog whistle through anti-dog-whistle rhetoric.

The structural isomorphism lies here: **the critique of the system's defects is recycled by the system as self-sustaining fuel.** The recursive dog whistle and *daotong* both turn "disease diagnosis" into "disease transmission." The more one says "dog whistles are destroying us," the stronger dog whistles become; the more one says "ritual and music are collapsing," the more entrenched ritual and music become.

## 4. Supplementary Analytical Dimensions

The four-morphism mapping above forms this paper's core framework. However, a complete analysis requires three supplementary dimensions — addressing asymmetries within the mapping.

### 4.1 Power Direction: Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

The four symbolic mechanisms of Zhou feudalism — *li*, *mingfen*, *bihui*, *daotong* — are **top-down instruments of control**. They serve an explicit hierarchical order, institutionalized and enforced by the ruling class. Even though *li* has "no author," it operates under the maintenance of the state apparatus and cultural elite.

Digital dog whistles — particularly political dog whistles on YouTube and social media — exhibit a **bottom-up emergent pattern**. Individual content creators, fan communities, and anonymous accounts spontaneously produce and decode dog whistles without centralized coordination. They are not "deployed" — they "grow" from group interaction.

This asymmetry is crucial: **the same structure, when the power direction is reversed, shifts function from "control" to "bonding," from "maintaining order" to "manufacturing opposition."** *Li* ensures subordinates know they are under the control of superiors; dog whistles make superiors feel they are resisting "the control from above."

### 4.2 Media Materiality: Bamboo Slips vs. Algorithmic Recommendation

Zhou ritual relied on a set of **material infrastructure**: bamboo slips, stone inscriptions, official academies, the imperial examination system, ancestral temple ceremonies — information transmission was slow, unidirectional, and required material qualification. To access the content of *li*, you needed to obtain bamboo slips, enter a school, and be taught the correct mode of interpretation. The medium itself was a filtering layer.

Dog whistles rely on **algorithmic distribution**: recommendation engines, comment section filtering, hashtag diffusion, personalized feeds. The algorithm does not perform qualification filtering — the algorithm performs **preference amplification**. The physical scarcity of bamboo slips ensured only a minority could participate in decoding; the informational abundance of algorithms ensures everyone is pushed before the content most suited to their decoding capacity.

This yields a key insight: **the "scarcity" of bamboo slips and the "abundance" of algorithms point toward the same effect — the information cocoon.** The Zhou Dynasty used physical thresholds to build cocoons (only those who studied the classics could participate); the digital age uses recommendation engines to build cocoons (each cocoon is customized, but the total number is infinite). The medium has shifted from scarcity to abundance, the cocoon from singular to plural, but the function of information isolation remains unchanged. To invoke McLuhan: "the medium is the message" — the dog whistle evolved negative-space and recursive variants precisely because the material properties of algorithmic media make "absence" itself transmissible content.

### 4.3 Explicit vs. Implicit: Public Code vs. Hidden Signal

This is the most easily overlooked asymmetry. **Zhou ritual is written explicitly in canonical texts** — anyone can read the *Rites of Zhou*. *Li* is effective not because it is hidden — on the contrary, **the power of *li* derives from its being public, transparent, and beyond question.** *Li* hides nothing — it openly declares the existence of order.

**Dog whistles in the digital age derive their effectiveness precisely from their implicitness and ambiguity.** Dog whistles have no canonical text, no explicit lexicon, no formal encoding rules. They operate on "those who know, know" — they are fluid, reflexive, and uncitable.

This means: **feudal society achieved the same control/bonding effect using "public code" that digital society achieves using "hidden signal."** The same structure, pursuing two opposite informational strategies to reach the identical end. This is not a contradiction — it is the adaptive expression of the same power logic across different media ecologies.

## 5. The Premodern Chinese Dog-Whistle Operating System

Western scholarship on dog whistles focuses almost exclusively on modern political communication contexts. Yet traditional Chinese culture possesses a complete, highly developed dog-whistle operating system — one that was not imported from the West, but evolved indigenously over three millennia as part of a native power-semiotic inheritance.

### 5.1 The *Chunqiu* Brushstroke: One Word, One Verdict

The "*Chunqiu* brushstroke" (*Chunqiu bifa*) is China's earliest institutionalized dog-whistle system. When Confucius compiled the *Spring and Autumn Annals*, he used different verbs for the same event — the death of the Son of Heaven, the death of a feudal lord, the death of a commoner. *Beng* (崩, "collapse"), *hong* (薨, "expire"), *zu* (卒, "end"), *si* (死, "die") — these four words describe the same biological event (cessation of breath), yet the reader receives entirely different political verdicts.

The Son of Heaven's death uses *beng* — not for its precision in describing physical death, but to mark whose death is worth the world stopping. The commoner's death uses *zu* — same fact, different semantic rank.

The frequency distribution of these four terms in the *Zuo Zhuan* provides quantitative evidence for this system:

| Term | Frequency (in "death" sense) | Primary Subjects |
|------|----------------------------|------------------|
| *beng* (崩) | 22 (13 for Son of Heaven) | Son of Heaven, Queen |
| *hong* (薨) | 32 | Only Lu State rulers and consorts |
| *zu* (卒) | 183 | Feudal lords, ministers |
| *si* (死) | 217 | Anyone except the Son of Heaven |

Four words, one biological event, systematically allocated to different ranks. More telling still: *hong* is used exclusively for Lu State rulers — non-Lu feudal lords who died are downgraded to *zu* in the text. The Lu annalist was defending his own state's symbolic privilege: a foreign lord, in these pages, does not deserve *hong*.

This is the precursor to the negative-space dog whistle: **different wording encodes the same event into different signals, providing different levels of meaning to different audience groups.** The commoner reads a record; the scholar-official reads a judgment. "Confucius compiled the *Spring and Autumn Annals*, and disorderly ministers and rebellious sons were terrified" — not because Confucius said "you are a disorderly minister," but because the disorderly minister, upon reading the imprint of that word, confirmed: **he knows who I am.**

### 5.2 *Weiyan Dayi*: Encoding High-Density Signals in Low Information Density

*Weiyan dayi* — "sparse words, profound meaning" — uses minimal language to carry maximal political information and moral judgment. This is the Chinese version of the hollow dog whistle: the sparser the surface text, the heavier the decoded meaning.

Confucius' principle of *shu er bu zuo* — "transmitting without creating" — claims he is merely relaying the ancients' words, not producing anything new. This is not humility; it is **encoding new political judgments as classical commentary.** "I said nothing new — I merely placed this passage of the classic in this position." The position of placement is itself the signal. The same move as the YouTuber saying "I'm just quoting someone" — the choice of what to quote is the stance; no addition is needed.

### 5.3 *Zhisang Mahuai*: The Definition of the Dog Whistle Itself

*Zhisang mahuai* — "pointing at the mulberry tree while cursing the locust tree" — this idiom is itself a perfect case of the dog whistle. The very existence of this phrase in the Chinese lexicon means: **the Chinese were already practicing this operation for centuries before coining the idiom that named it.** No need for Western academia to teach you what a dog whistle is — your own cultural language already named it.

### 5.4 *Chenwei*: The Authorless Dog Whistle, Upgraded

*Chenwei* — celestial omens, natural disasters, auspicious signs — is the Chinese version of a primordial "tweet delivery system." A solar eclipse appears in the sky — this is not an astronomical observation report. The eclipse means "Heaven is inclined to disagree with your governance." The official bears no risk for saying "I oppose you" — because the speaker is not him; the speaker is "Heaven."

*Chenwei* is the ultimate political realization of the authorless dog whistle: **the signal's "sender" is displaced to a dimension beyond accountability — Heaven — while the "decoders" (officials and the emperor) must recognize the signal's validity.** To deny the meaning of an eclipse is to deny the entire cosmic order.

### 5.5 The *Bihui* System: Institutionalized Silence

Li Shimin's name contained the character *shi* (世), so that character could no longer be written — every document in the empire substituted an alternate glyph or left a missing stroke. No one in the empire said "I fear the emperor." But every single *bihui* substitution was the precise transmission of that signal.

This is the negative-space dog whistle fully institutionalized: **not merely "not saying" — but "not saying" codified into law, transformed into a philological standard, and executed as a universally enforced rule.** Digital platform *bihui* is temporary and spontaneous; Chinese imperial *bihui* was codified, compulsory, and perfected over a millennium of institutional refinement. But the underlying structure is identical: silence is used to confirm the subject's position within the order.

### 5.6 *Tuogu Gaizhi*: Reform by Dog Whistle

The most thorough reformers in Chinese history — from Wang Anshi to Kang Youwei — never declared "I am reforming." What they declared was: "We are restoring the institutions of the ancient sage-kings."

This is the recursive dog whistle operating in a premodern political context: **the political intent of reform is encoded within the narrative of "restoring antiquity."** "I am recovering the Way of Yao and Shun" as a means to legitimize "abolishing the current system." Reformists and conservatives receive different semantic layers of the same sentence: reformists read "reform," conservatives read "restore" — and on the literal level, there is no contradiction. By the time the decoder recognizes "antiquity-restoration" as a rhetorical function, the reform has already been executed.

### 5.7 Chan Koans: The Hollow Dog Whistle, Religious Edition

"What is the Buddha?"
"A dried shit-stick."

This is not nonsense — this is precisely a perfect annotation of the hollow dog whistle. Surface gibberish, a literally absurd answer — noise to the outsider, signal to the insider. Chan koans prove: **under conditions of extreme low information density (or even anti-information — "dried shit-stick"), signal validity is not merely preserved; it is amplified.**

### 5.8 Confucius' "Rectification of Names": The Founding Manifesto of the Hollow Dog Whistle

"If names are not correct, speech will not be in accordance; if speech is not in accordance, affairs will not be accomplished."

Confucius' *zhengming* (rectification of names) argument is the founding theoretical statement of the hollow dog whistle. The name-position itself — the word *jun* (ruler) relative to the word *chen* (minister) — issues no specific command. But once names are rectified, speech becomes orderly, affairs succeed. The core logic of *zhengming*: **you do not need to tell people what to do — you need only to first establish where everyone stands.** This is the earliest documented explicit theoretical articulation of the hollow dog whistle's operational mechanism.

### 5.9 Mencius' "I Had No Choice": The Rhetorical Patent for the Recursive Dog Whistle

"How could I be fond of argument? I simply have no choice."

Mencius packages an active political attack as a "forced response," completing the recursive dog-whistle maneuver. His active intervention is encoded as: "I am not attacking you — I am forced to respond in defense of the Way of the Former Kings." Every component of this sentence is a dog whistle: the first layer says "I am not the aggressor" to avoid accountability; the second layer says "the Way of the Former Kings" to signal in-group membership; the third layer says "I have no choice" to imply "this is your fault for making me say this." Mencius, twenty-three centuries ago, invented the entire rhetorical toolkit of the modern political YouTuber.

## 6. Case Studies

The following three cases illustrate the concrete operation of the four-morphism mapping on contemporary digital platforms.

### 6.1 YouTube Political Commentators: "I'm Just Asking Questions"

The widely used "I'm just asking questions" on American political YouTube is a **recursive dog whistle of the *tuogu gaizhi* type**. The creator does not declare a position — he is "just asking a question." This operation extracts the claim from the content layer: "I am not asserting anything, I am merely raising a question" — but the wording of the question, the selected evidence fragments, and the direction of inquiry collectively constitute a complete positional statement. This shares the structure of *tuogu gaizhi*: the reformer does not announce "I am reforming," only "I am restoring ancient institutions." Surface de-assertion, with substantive assertion already encoded in the direction of inquiry. What the target audience decodes is not a question — it is a position.

### 6.2 TikTok Conspiracy Communities: High-Frequency Hashtag Substitution

The conspiracy theory community on TikTok swaps one hashtag for another — as the old one is banned, a new one emerges — with a surface-innocuous tag like #justforfun becoming a new signal carrier within the community. This is an **authorless dog whistle** in operation: no one decides "what the next code word is"; the group self-organizes to find the new encoding. This matches the transmission pattern of *li* — not promulgated by anyone, but emerging from the group. The hashtag itself contains no policy-violating content, yet the information it carries is complete within the specific decoding community. Algorithmic hashtag banning can never keep pace with community recoding — because the hashtag is already abandoned before it gets banned.

### 6.3 Twitter/X: Retweet as Endorsement (+ Negative Space)

A common operation on Twitter — retweeting a post with zero added commentary — is a **stacked deployment of the hollow dog whistle and the negative-space dog whistle.** "I said nothing" is negative-space (silence as signal); "but the moment I retweet this to you, you already know what I am saying" is the hollow dog whistle (position recognition as content). This operation has no content — only a positional marker. The retweet is a pure *mingfen* act: it does not transmit information; it confirms "I stand on the same side as you."

### 6.4 Institutional Dog Whistles from a U.S. Government Agency (2025)

In August 2025, the official X account of the U.S. Department of Homeland Security posted an ICE recruitment advertisement captioned "Which way, American man?" superimposed on a 1936 political cartoon. Critics — including the Anti-Defamation League — immediately identified the phrase as an echo of the 1978 white supremacist text *Which Way, Western Man?* Earlier, the same account had capitalized "HH" in the phrase "Remember your Homeland's Heritage" — interpreted as a shorthand for "Heil Hitler."

This is a **stacked institutional deployment of the recursive dog whistle and the hollow dog whistle.** DHS's defense followed the negative-space dog-whistle logic precisely: "It is just a historical cartoon" — retreating meaning to the literal layer while denying any encoding intent. But the target audience received a complete in-group confirmation: "We know what you are saying, and you know we know." A government agency — not a fringe influencer — is executing the same rhetorical structure as Mencius' "I had no choice": a surface-innocuous recruitment advertisement with an inner layer transmitting exclusive identity confirmation to a specific group. The dog whistle's sender has migrated from the social periphery to the center of the state apparatus — the latest evolution of the power direction dynamic (§4.1) in the 2020s.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper's core argument: **the digital dog whistle is not a new communication phenomenon — it is the re-instantiation of feudal power-semiotic systems on digital media.** Through a one-to-one structural mapping of four dog-whistle variants onto four Zhou ritual-symbolic mechanisms, supplemented by three analytical dimensions and evidence from the premodern Chinese dog-whistle operating system, this paper demonstrates that the two systems share a common underlying power logic.

The conclusion carries two main implications:

**For dog-whistle research:** The effort to "solve" or "detect" dog whistles — whether through NLP or platform policy — faces a fundamental obstacle that is not technical in nature. What the dog whistle evades is not algorithms — it is **the structural features of power semiotics itself.** The moderation system is racing against an opponent that completed its evolution three millennia ago.

**For the study of power semiotics:** Feudalism was not "eliminated" and then "replaced" by representative democracy — its core symbolic mechanisms never disappeared. They merely changed media. When a YouTuber transmits a political stance through "I'm just asking questions," he is executing the same rhetorical operation that Mencius patented twenty-three centuries ago. Not "unconsciously." Not "coincidentally." It is **the same power-semiotic logic running on different material substrates.**

The ceiling of digital dog-whistle evolution is not the limit of AI detection capability — it is the power semiotics finalized three thousand years ago. No matter how the medium changes, the grammar of power had already been written.

## Methodological Note

This paper is a conceptual analysis rather than an empirical study. Its validity rests on the precision of structural mapping and the typicality of historical cases, not on statistical significance. The four-morphism framework and supplementary dimensions are presented in service of theory construction — establishing a testable structural alignment between communication studies and power semiotics. Empirical verification — including systematic content analysis of contemporary dog-whistle instances, corpus annotation of historical texts, and controlled experiments on audience decoding — constitutes the natural next step following the framework's articulation, to be undertaken in subsequent research.
